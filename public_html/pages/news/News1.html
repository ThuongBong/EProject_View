16 ancient works are still strong ahead of time
Posted on October 11,2017 by admin
-The massive buildings were built by hand, without the help of machines, trailers, even metal tools but with a thousand years of vitality.

Machu Picchu: Located in the high mountains of Peru, the Inca city was discovered by American historian Hiram Bingham in 1911. It is hidden in the jungles of the Spanish conquest of South America. Since it was discovered, Machu Picchu has become one of the most important tourist attractions in Peru, the most famous symbol of the Inca world.

Pompii: Once a vibrant city in Italy, Pompii was destroyed and buried under lava and ash, when volcano Vesuvius erupted. Today, there are remains of fish markets, bakeries, public baths, and several villas.

Pyramid of Giza: Located in the desert south of Cairo, Egypt, three huge pyramids rise from the sand. The highest peak is Khufu, also known as the Great Pyramid, and is also the oldest of the seven wonders of the ancient world. The large sphinx statues were built in the pyramid, which is considered a protective mascot for the pharaohs and queens.

Teotihuacan: Located in northeastern Mexico City, this is one of the largest pyramids and is preserved intact pre-Columbian. Teotihuacan has a pyramid of suns and lunar. In 1987, UNESCO recognized the city of Teotihuacan as a World Heritage Site.

Roman Square: In the 5th century BC, the Roman Forum was the administrative, religious, political and commercial center of Rome. This is where parliaments discuss the law, hold court hearings, rituals, prayers, or funerals for important dignitaries.

La Maison: The Colosseum is located on the west side of the Roman square, with a population of 80,000. It also organizes battles for gladiators, executions, hunting animals and other events. The Colosseum has long been considered a symbol of the empire and one of the most beautiful Roman architectural remains.

Great Wall: Built from the Qin Dynasty to protect northern China from foreign invaders, the 20,000-square-mile wall, estimated to have about 25,000 watchtowers.

Borobudur: This is the great Buddhist pyramid in central Java, Indonesia. Borobudur was built around the 8th-9th centuries under the Sailendra dynasty, which is devoted to Buddhism. After the fall of Sailendra Buddhism, the area was abandoned and forgotten during the 10th century. In 1814, a delegation of European scientists, by the Dutch colonial government in Indonesia dispatched to new research conducted and restored the temple. The structure of the map consists of 12 large, open, square, alternating, alternating, overlapping platforms forming a 42 m high block. The steps from level 1 to level 9 covered the reliefs, ornate, described the life of Buddha Sakyamuni, Bodhisattvas and enlightened beings Buddhism, and both scenes on nirvana or in hell ...

Ephesus: Located in Turkey, it started as a commercial port on the coast of Ionia. Ephesus was built in the 10th century BC. The city became prosperous after being controlled by the Roman Republic in 129 BC. Estimatively, Ephesus had a population of 33,600-56,000 during Roman times, and was the third largest city in Asia Minority after Sardis and Alexandria Troas.

Tikal: Mayan ruins in Guatemala are famous for their beautiful and ancient temples. In 1979, UNESCO recognized this as a World Heritage Site. Although today Tikal looks like only stone and stone, the temple was formerly green and red from the mortar mix. The Maya building was completely without metal tools, animal support or carts, and was abandoned in 900 AD.

Acropolis: The name means "Athena's defense," the building in Athens, Greece is the most famous defense in the world. The Acropolis includes Athena Nike, Propylaea and Erechtheion. Built between 461-429 BC, it represents the wealth and power of the ancient Greeks.

Petra: Located in Jordan, between the Red Sea and the Dead Sea, the ancient city of Petra was carved by the Nabateans, an ancient Semitic tribe carved into the rocky mountains. The site was discovered in 1812, including temples, churches and more than 800 graves.

Moai: Easter Island, Chile is one of the islands famous for its sculptures of Polynesian moai dating back to 1250-1500 AD. All statues are made of monolithic stone. The largest moai ever built was Paro, up to 10 m high and weighing 75 tons. A statue was found in the unfinished state at a height of 21 m and weighed 270 tons.

Stonehenge: This ruined place in England is always one of the secrets of the world. Excavations show that Stonehenge was also a burial place for a while. It is also believed to be a religious place due to the solar cycle. Each stone weighs up to 45 tons, stacked on top of each other without using a trailer.

Chichen Itza: Located in the northern part of the Yucatan Peninsula, Chichen Itza is one of the largest cities in the Mayan region with an area of ​​over 5 square kilometers. In addition to the undulating temples, there are also saunas, polished and natural pits.

Angkor Wat Temple is considered the largest religious monument in the world, covering an area of ​​over 400 km2. The main temple was built by King Suryavarman II from 1113 to 1150 and today appears on the Cambodian flag.
