History of the Machu picchu

Machu Picchu is a pre-Columbian Inca ruin in good conservation condition at an altitude of 2,430 m (7,970 ft) on a pointed mountain. Machu Picchu is located in the Urubamba Valley in Peru, about 70 km (44 miles) northwest of Cusco. Having been forgotten for centuries by the outside world, Machu Picchu has returned to the attention of the world thanks to archaeologist Hiram Bingham who rediscovered this place five years ago. 1911, and wrote a book selling very well about it. Peru is currently carrying out legal action to reclaim thousands of Bingham objects that have been taken from the site.

History:



Machu Picchu is perhaps the most familiar symbol of the Inca Empire. Often referred to as the "Lost City of the Incas", since 1983 the site has been selected as a UNESCO World Heritage Site.



It is believed that the city was built by Sapa Inca Pachacuti in around 1440, and remained uninhabited until the Spanish conquest of Peru in 1532. Archaeological evidence (plus studies The Machu Picchu is not a typical city, but rather a resort town of the Inca nobility (similar to the Roman villages). This place has a large palace and temples dedicated to the Inca gods surrounding a courtyard, with other buildings for the servants.

Location:



Machu Picchu is 70 km northwest of Cusco, on the top of Machu Picchu, at an altitude of about 2,350 meters above sea level. It is one of the most important archaeological centers in South America and is therefore the largest tourist attraction in Peru. From the top, at the cliffs of Machu Picchu, is a 600-meter long escarpment. the bottom of the Urubamba River. The city location is a military secret due to the steep cliffs and surrounding mountains that are excellent natural military defenses.

Architecture:

All works at Machu Picchu follow the Inca architectural style with unused mortar walls and equally sized stones. The Inca are the master of this technique, called block stone, in which blocks are cut so that they can be grafted together tightly without the need for mortar. Many junctions are so perfect that they can not even squeeze a blade between the stones.



The Inca never used the wheel. How they put large blocks of stone is still a mystery, although in general people believe that they used hundreds of people to push the rocks up. We still do not know why the Inca did not leave any documentation about the construction by the script they used, called Khipu (Quipu), yet to be decoded.
