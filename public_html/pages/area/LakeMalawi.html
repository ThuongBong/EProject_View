Lake Malawi (also known as Lake Nyasa in many countries, or Lake Nyassa, Lake Niassa, or Lago Niassa in Mozambique), is a lake in the Great Lakes of Africa. This lake is located in the southernmost part of the Great Barrier Reef in East Africa. It is the third largest lake in Africa and is the eighth largest lake in the world and also the second deepest lake in Africa. It is located between Malawi, Mozambique and Tanzania.



Geography:Lake Malawi (or Lake Nyaza) ranges in length from 560 to 580 kilometers, the largest of which is about 75 kilometers. The total area of the lake is about 29,600 square kilometers (11,429 sq mi). Lake shorelines are located in western Mozambique, eastern Malawi and southern Tanzania. The largest river flowing into this lake is the Ruhuhu River. This large freshwater lake has an outlet at the southern end of the Shire River, a tributary flowing into the huge Zambezi River in Mozambique.
Lake Malawi is located in the Great Rift Valley created by the opening of the East Africa Dispersal Zone, where the African plateau is divided into two parts. This is called a different plate boundary. The formation of Lake Malawi (or Nyaza) is estimated to vary from about 40,000 years. Or about 1 to 2 million years ago.

Lake Nyasa or Lake Malawi:The geographic name of this lake is also controversial. Malawi calls it "Lake Malawi", while most other countries (especially Tanzania) and international maps call it Lake Nyasa. The origin of the name dispute is geopolitical controversy that began before Malawi became independent in 1964, when the territory was called "Nyasaland".

The troubles continued to occur for various political reasons in the 1960s, when Malawian President Hastings Banda was the only African leader to establish diplomatic relations with South Africa governed by whites. . Recognition of South Africa's rule was dismissed by most African leaders, including President Julius Nyerere of Tanzania. The contradiction in the policies towards South Africa has led to greater controversy between Malawi and Tanzania, particularly with regard to the name of the lake and the waters boundary between the two countries.
