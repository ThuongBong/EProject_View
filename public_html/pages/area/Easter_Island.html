History of the Easter Island

Easter Island is an island in the southeastern Pacific Ocean, under Chilean sovereignty, located at the extreme southeastern Polynesia triangle. Easter Island is famous for 887 stone statues, called moai, created by ancient Rapa Nui. In 1995, UNESCO recognized Easter Island as a World Heritage Site, with most of its protected area in the Rapa Nui National Park.



Easter Island is one of the most remote islands in the world. The nearest island is Pitcairn Island (about 50 people in 2013), 2,075 kilometers (1,289 mi) away, the nearest town is Rikitea, having a population of just over 500, located on Mangareva Island, 2,606 km (1,619 mi) away; The nearest landmark is in central Chile, 3,512 kilometers (2,182 mi). Easter Island is a special territory of Chile, incorporated in 1888. In administrative terms, it belongs to the Valparaíso region, and, more precisely, it is the only commune in the province of Isla de Pascua. According to 2012 statistics, 5,800 people, of which about 60% are native Rapa Nui.



Moai are statues carved from volcanic ash at Rapa Nui, Easter Island, Chile. All statues are made of monolithic stone, meaning that they are carved from a single platform. The largest moai ever built was "Paro", as high as 10 meters (33 feet) and weighing 75 tons. A statue was found in an unfinished state up to 21 meters (69 ft) and weighed 270 tons.



Less than one-fifth of the statues were moved to ritual sites and erected when a red rock pukau was worn. These "hats", as they are often called, are made of stone in a single mine, Puna Pau. The legends of the island people talk about a patriarch named Hotu Matu'a, who left his hometown to find a new home. The place he chose is now called Easter Island. When he died, the island was split up by his six sons and later divided further by his nephews. The people on the island may have believed their statues could absorb the "mana" (supernatural powers) of the leader. They may believe that by focusing on the island mana the good omen will come, for example, the rain will fall and the seasons will bumper.
