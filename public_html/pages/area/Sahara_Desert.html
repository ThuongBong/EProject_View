History of the sahara desert

Sahara is the world's largest desert, the third largest desert on Earth (after Antarctica and the Arctic Circle), covering an area of over 9,000,000 km², approximately the size of the United States and China. Sahara in northern Africa and up to 2.5 million years old.

Overview:The sahara desert is surrounded by the Atlantic Ocean in the west, the Atlas Mountains and the Mediterranean Sea in the north, the Red Sea (Red Sea) and Egypt in the east; Sudan and the Niger River valley in the south. The highest point in the desert is Mount Emi Koussi at an elevation of 3415 m above sea level, in the Tibesti Mountains in northern Tchad.
Throughout the Ice Age, the Sahara has been much wetter today. And there are also many species of plants and animals living here. Nowadays, except for the Nile Valley, it is possible to grow a lot of vegetables and a few other places, such as the northern highlands, near the Mediterranean where olive trees can be grown, and most of these areas can not grow.

Climate history:Sahara has undergone tremendous changes between wet and dry for several hundred thousand years. In the last ice age, the Sahara is larger than today, stretching further south than the present boundary. The end of the ice age brought about a more humid period for the Sahara, from around 8000 BC to 6000 BC, probably because of the low pressure zones across the collapsed ice sheets to the north.
When the ice sheets are gone, the northern Sahara begins to dry. However, not long after the end of the ice sheets, the current monsoon rains to the Sahel blew farther north and clashed with the dry south of the Sahara. Monsoon in Africa (and elsewhere) appears due to summer heat. The air on the continent becomes hotter and rises, pulling the moist and cold air from the sea. This phenomenon causes rain.



History of the cattle stage:By 6000 BC Egyptian dynasties in southwestern Egypt learned to breed cattle and build large houses. Living in fixed settlements, organized in pre-Egyptian pre-Egyptian society in the mid-sixth millennium BC, the focus was on cereals and agricultural cattle: cattle, goats, pigs and lamb. The metal utensils replaced the previous stone appliances. Tanneries, pottery and textiles have also emerged during this period. There are indications of Al Fayyum's seasonal or temporary occupancy in the sixth millennium BC, with food-seeking activities focused primarily on fishing, hunting and gathering. Arrowheads, knives and scraper stones are numerous. Burial objects such as pots, jewelry, agricultural tools and hunting and food such as dried meat and fruit. The dead are buried facing west.



Berber Stage:The Phoenicians created a union of kingdoms across the entire Sahara to Egypt, which was generally located on the coast, but also in some cases in the desert.
By 2500 BC Sahara had become as dry as this day and became an inescapable wall for man, only scattered some settlements around the oases, but the trade and commerce across the desert has not yet appeared. One of the major exceptions is the Nile Valley. However, the Nin River has many irreversible falls that make it difficult to trade and exchange
At some point between 633 and 530 BC, the Hanno navy established or strengthened the Phenicia colonies in the Western Sahara, but all traces of the past were lost and there was virtually no trace left. what See History of Western Sahara.



Greek:By 500 BC new influences from Greece and Phenicia reached this area. Greek traders traveled along the east coast of the desert, setting up commercial areas along the Red Sea coast. Carthaginians have explored the desert's Atlantic coast. Due to lack of water and markets, human footprints never exceeded southern Morocco.



Civilized city:An urban civilization, Garamantes, developed during this period in the center of the Sahara, in a valley now known as Wadi al-Ajal in Fazzan, Libya. The Garamantes civilization develops through the canals through the valleys of the valley to the mountains leading water into the fields.
The Greeks and Romans knew of the Garamantes civilization and regarded them as nomadic nomads. However, they still traded with the Garamantes, and a Roman bath was found in Garamantes's Garama capital.

Arabs:The greatest change in Sahara history to the Arab conquest itself has brought camels into this area. For the first time an effective trans-Saharan trade network can be implemented. The Sahel dynasties, especially the Ghana Empire and the later Mali Empire, became powerful and wealthy by exporting gold and salt to North Africa.
This trading system has existed for centuries until European development and maritime technical development allowed the ships, originally from Portugal but soon after the whole of Western Europe, Travel around the desert and collect resources in Guinea. Sahara quickly fell into isolation.
The colonial powers also did not pay much attention to this area, but in modern times some mines and communities have been developed to exploit the natural resources of the desert. These resources include large quantities of petroleum and natural gas minerals in Algeria and Libya as well as large amounts of phosphate minerals in Morocco and Western Sahara.
